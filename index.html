01 — About

Hello! I'm Advaith.

I'm a medical student at Emory School of Medicine, working to become a neurosurgeon-scientist at the intersection of clinical practice and technology. My goal is to work alongside a team of researchers, physicians, patients, biomedical engineers, IP specialists, health economists, lawyers, and medical anthropologists to neuroengineer brain-based technologies that I can help facilitate into neurosurgical practice. I'm especially interested in how doctors who can think like an engineer and an anthropologist can reinvigorate existing brain-technology paradigms — and what an aspiring neurosurgeon who likes to build cool things and think a lot can uniquely contribute to this niche space.

I graduated from Tulane in May 2026 with degrees in Asian Studies and Political Economy. Outside those degrees, I did a ton of research. My honors thesis examined British colonization of Indian health infrastructure and cross-cultural approaches to neuropsychiatry and spiritual experience in South India. I also conducted primary cilia stress-response research at the Tulane Brain Institute, gave a TEDxTulane talk on that subject, and studied (and oftentimes published) across tuberculosis diagnostics, pregnancy disorders, and computational and theoretical chemistry. Having that breadth of experience — across both the sciences and liberal arts — allowed me to oftentimes engage with the dichotomous nature of medicine – science and religion, theory and practice, individuals and systems. I (humbly) think it made me a sharper writer, a better communicator, and more attuned to how scientific progress actually gets applied to patient populations.

Outside of all the school stuff, I sing Carnatic music, am learning the guitar, like to lift heavy things one hour a day weightlifting, go running, spend a lot of time trying new restaurants & coffee shops, and travel everywhere — usually to try the restaurants.

02 — Research

Laboratory & Field Work

Tasker Lab — Tulane Brain Institute

Neuroscience

My core wet-lab research at Tulane investigated the role of neuronal primary cilia in modulating stress responses in mice. That is — do neuronal primary cilia within corticotropin-releasing hormone neurons play a role in modulating the negative feedback loop of stress pathways? This work earned me the national Goldwater Scholarship — awarded to 1 of 441 students from over 5,000 nominees for commitment to a career in the medical sciences. In the lab I performed cryosectioning, immunohistochemistry, cardiac perfusion, PCR, gel electrophoresis, ELISA, submandibular blood collection, cell culturing, and ANOVA statistical testing, and I presented a poster over preliminary data findings.

Independent Research — Tulane School of Liberal Arts

Honors Thesis

For my honors thesis I received the Gordon Fellowship ($4,400), the Dale E. Miller & Hillary Bachmann Miller Fund, and the Frankel Family Undergraduate Research Fellowship. The thesis examined British colonization of Indian health infrastructure and explored cross-cultural approaches to neuropsychiatry and spiritual experience in South India. Alongside this work, I delivered a TEDxTulane talk, "Divine Madness in Neuropsychiatry," to 100+ attendees — presenting an original framework that bridges Asian Studies and neuroscience. [Check out the talk here! →]

Matthews Research Group — SMU Dept. of Chemistry

Computational Chemistry

I organized and led a team project modeling malathion binding to acetylcholinesterase to elucidate the molecular basis of organophosphate poisoning. Separately, I quantified the photostability of molecules to understand primordial ancestors of RNA nucleobases, and used Python and Bash scripting to test the efficacy of Tensor Hypercontraction (THC) software in replicating essential aspects of many-body chemical processes. This work earned Best Poster Presentation for undergraduates at the Southern Regional Meeting — top 10 of 80+ posters submitted.

McDowell & Nathavitharana Research Groups

Global Health

Working with these groups, I contributed to two major tuberculosis initiatives. For the World Health Organization, I conducted a thematic study and systematic review to help develop guidelines for diagnosing TB in high-burden countries. Through the National TB Coalition of America, I collaborated with physicians and researchers to establish evidence-based guidelines and restrictions for reducing pulmonary TB transmission. Our work was published in Clinical Infectious Diseases!

Tulane School of Public Health — Dept. of Epidemiology

Epidemiology

I evaluated the quality and content of international clinical guidelines on hypertensive disorders of pregnancy using the AGREE II instrument, identifying best practices for treating these conditions across health systems. I also organized and led a project investigating how institutional Human Research Protection Program (HRPP) policies may inadvertently contribute to the underrepresentation of health disparity populations in clinical research.

03 — Community Work

I also love to work with youth in my communities!

Here are some other side projects I've worked on with other students and youth over the years.

Cracking the Wonder Code (CTWC)

I founded CTWC to give kids in underserved communities access to STEAM education through hands-on robotics camps. Over the years I grew it to reach 130+ students across 4 states and 2 continents — most of whom had never had access to that kind of programming before.

crackingthewondercode.org →

AAPI Mentorship Program — Tulane

I started and directed a mentorship initiative that brought together Tulane's Asian American students to mentor local AAPI high schoolers in New Orleans. We helped them work through their academics, prepping their college applications, and celebrating all kinds of Asian holidays, like Diwali, Lunar New Year, and Eid. It was important to me to build a pipeline that actually improved AAPI representation in my city, where that community often goes unseen.

Goldring Center for Culinary Medicine

I served as treasurer and helped organize workshops on nutrition and food science for New Orleans youth — teaching the basics of healthy eating and preventative medicine through food (we made burrito bowls with them and explained the phhysiology behind all the healthy ingredients in them). On campus, I also led club meetings and initiatives to build nutritional literacy among my peers.
